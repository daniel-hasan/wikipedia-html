{{Infobox Celebrity}} Susannah Caroline Constantine (born June 3, 1962)[1] is an award-winning English fashion guru, style advisor, presenter, bestselling fashion author and designer.

Constantine was born into a wealthy family, where her father was successful in property and shipping sectors. She was privately educated as a child and went on to date British royalty, David, Viscount Linley, during the 1980s. Constantine has been involved in fashion for a long period, originally working in America for Giorgio Armani and then John Galliano in London. She met Trinny Woodall in 1994 who both proceeded to write a weekly fashion column, Ready to Wear. They founded Ready2shop.com, a dot-com fashion advice business, and wrote their first fashion advice book in 2000, Ready 2 Dress, both of which failed. From there they were commissioned to BBC Two to host the style series, What Not to Wear, from 2001 to 2005. She made regular appearances as a style advisor on The Oprah Winfrey Show and following her success on the shows, she went on to co-host Trinny & Susannah Undress... on ITV in 2006 and [[Undress the Nation] in 2007. Constantine and Woodall have now dressed over 5,000 women.

She has co-written many fashion advice book with Woodall, some of which have become bestsellers in the United Kingdom and United States. Her second book, entitled What Not to Wear, has won her a prestigious British Book Award[2] and sold 670,000 copies.[3] It is estimated that her various style advice books have sold 2.5 million copies in Britain and the United States.[4] Constantine and Woodall have designed their own clothing range for Littlewoods which made its debut in 2007, followed by the release of their latest fashion advice book, The Body Shape Bible.

Education

Constantine was privately educated at boarding schools including Queen's Gate School in South Kensington, London[5] and the £20,000-per-year St Mary's School in Wantage, Oxfordshire which was run by nuns.[6] Her time at St Mary's School was described by herself as "miserable" due to her homesickness and insecurity, meaning she was not able to make friends.[6]

In mid 2007, Constantine spoke about how she received a letter from St Mary's School, inviting her to come back to the school to talk about her career and success to current pupils. Constantine immediately declined the offer with a four-letter refusal and wrote "No fucking way" on the letter she had received.[7] Her personal assistant was then left to deal with the situation, who wrongly sent the inviting letter with the hand-written sentence by Constantine, back to the school. The boarding school then wrote a shocked letter back, explaining that they will not let Constantine near the school again.[5][6]

Early career

Constantine originally did a year of Montessori training after she had left school but she then moved onto other projects[5] such as studying sculpture in Brussels.[8] Constantine had taught children for three years[9] and also worked as a style consultant for Harrods.[5][10] Constantine's love for horses had inspired her to become a mounted policewoman in London, but was deterred when she was required to do four years walking the beat.[11] She wrote a book about present giving, which prompted The Telegraph to write an unpleasant article implying she had never done a day's work in her life, something which deeply upset her.[12]

She is now best-known as a fashion guru and style advisor, where her fashion career started when she worked for many years under fashion designer Giorgio Armani as a shop girl in America.[9] She later came back to London with the hope of securing a job with Armani but ended up working for designers such as Richard James, Patrick Cox,[8] Alistair Blair and John Galliano which gained her an even greater understanding of fashion, providing an in depth knowledge of how a garment progresses from a sketch.[9] She then started working with the British Brain and Spine Foundation and consequently met the sports editor of The Daily Telegraph.[9] Whilst doing a piece for GMTV, he asked Constantine to report the women's World Cup Final in cricket on finding out that it was her passion. She proceeded to write about cars and then fashion.[9]

She teamed up in 1994 with Trinny Woodall after they had met at a party hosted by David, Viscount Linley.[10] The pair who did not like each other at first, proceeded to write Ready to Wear, a weekly style guide for The Daily Telegraph which ran successfully for seven years.[10] The column promoted affordable high-street fashion and they used themselves to illustrate which clothing suited which figure.[5] Constantine and Woodall also became the co-founders of Ready2shop.com, a dot-com fashion advice business that ceased trading after running out of funding in November 2000. After the business venture had failed, investors lost a reputed £10 million.[13] It was during their time running the internet business that they had a serious argument that almost ended their friendship.[14]

Constantine made her television debut when Granada Sky Broadcasting signed her and Woodall to present a daytime shopping show named after their column in the Daily Telegraph called Ready to Wear, and they soon released their first fashion advice book, Ready 2 Dress in 2000.[10] The book was unsuccessful and resulted in the pulping of 13,000 copies.[15] Soon after the start of their television career, they secured a frequent makeover slot on the show Richard & Judy. It ensured that they had further exposure in television and gained attention from Jane Root, controller of BBC Two, who took a risk and signed them up after their book and their internet business had been unmitigated disasters.[10]

Television

2001—2003

Constantine began co-hosting What Not to Wear with Trinny Woodall in 2001, which required using her fashion advice and expertise in order to reform participants' appearances and fashion style. Constantine and Woodall hosted What Not to Wear until 2005 where they instantly became renowned for their tactile behaviour with the participants, direct advice,[16] and frequently referring to breasts as tits which has become somewhat of a trademark.[17] Never one to hold back, a notorious moment arose when Constantine spontaneously pulled a female candidate's underwear down during filming as her knicker line was visible.[18] Critics of What Not to Wear argued that the duo were too patronising to their subjects, a claim which they strongly disagree with.[8] The show made Constantine and Woodall household names and are now known together as Trinny and Susannah. They have stated that their relationship is more like a marriage and that being total opposites, they balance each other out.[11] They cannot imagine working without each other,[11] and have even considered taking insurance out on their partnership should something unforeseen happen.[14]

Constantine (right) and Woodall on What Not to Wear.

Constantine has the belief that "anyone can achieve style. It doesn't matter who you are or where you're from."[17] She also says that she finds dressing other women easy, but finds it difficult when dressing herself.[18]

During her time co-hosting What Not to Wear for five series, she and Woodall gained recognition for their work on the show, winning a Royal Television Society Award in 2002 for being the best factual presenters.[19] In 2002, Constantine provided Jeremy Clarkson and Lesley Joseph with makeovers on a celebrity version of What Not to Wear.[20] After Clarkson appeared on the show, he stated "I'd rather eat my own hair than shop with these two [Constantine and Woodall] again".[17] Adding to its success, the show was nominated for the Features Award at the BAFTAS in both 2002 and 2003.[21]

For charity, in 2002 during the BBC's Children in Need programme, both Constantine and Woodall performed their own version of Madonna's hit single "Vogue" with a group of celebrity backing singers.[22] They became the faces of Nescafé in 2003 in which they were featured in television advertisements promoting the brand of coffee. As part of their contract, Constantine and Woodall gave a Nescafé competition winner a £10,000 makeover.[23] In the same year, Constantine did special What Not to Wear episode in which she transformed the appearances of Jo Brand and Sophie Raworth to attend a BAFTA Award ceremony.

2004—2005

Following a ratings success, What Not to Wear was promoted from BBC Two to BBC One in 2004 to be received by even larger audiences. The format was changed slightly, where there was a 60 minute show with two makeovers instead of a 30 minute show with only one makeover[24] and also saw Constantine spending a day as one of her subjects. What Not to Wear soon gained her international fame when it was aired in countries such as Spain and Portugal as well as in the American continent.

She appeared again on Children in Need in 2004, which included a special segment with her giving the fictional EastEnders characters Little Mo and Mo Harris a makeover à la What Not to Wear, commenting on them with her usual "no nonsense" approach.[25] She also appeared on Comic Relief: Red Nose Night Live 05. In 2005, Constantine was a participant on the live BBC coverage of The Royal Wedding of Charles and Camilla, where she contributed as a fashion and contemporary social commentator.[26] In that same year, Constantine voiced a robot version of herself in the science fiction series, Doctor Who. In the episode "Bad Wolf", Trine-e and Zu-Zana were two robots acting out a deadly futuristic version of What not to Wear, offering a makeover to Jack Harkness in a most gruesome form.[27]

The Oprah Winfrey Show has also seen her and Woodall appearing regularly as a makeover and style experts, where they gave fashion advice and guidelines on how to better overall appearances and giving numerous American women fashion makeovers.[28][29] They also did an "Oprah bra and swimsuit intervention".[30]

2006—present

In 2006, Constantine defected from the BBC to ITV for a deal worth a reputed £1.2 million[14] to start a new show, Trinny & Susannah Undress..., on October 3, 2006. Since the transfer to ITV, What Not to Wear on BBC One has been hosted by Lisa Butcher and Mica Paris. The second series of Trinny & Susannah Undress... aired on ITV in June 2007, and it maintained the format of series one which saw Constantine and Woodall advising couples who were finding problems within their marriage.[31] The advice and a fashion makeover aimed to make the troubled relationships stronger. They were also excited about the new task of combating men's fashion issues.[32]

Constantine (right) and Woodall on Trinny & Susannah Undress.
Constantine stated in an interview that filming the show was very emotionally draining, and as a result, she often went home crying.[5] The programme did not come without its critics who questioned the depth at which Constantine and Woodall could deal with serious issues raised during the programme.[5] Their straight-talking advice however, ensured that they were voted the ninth most terrifying celebrities on television in a 2006 Radio Times poll consisting of 3,000 people.[33] On October 16 2006, they both appeared on NBC's The Today Show on which they performed makeovers on three women especially for the show. They also promoted their fashion book, Trinny & Susannah Take on America.[34]

The new seires on ITV sees a change in format and explores the main fashion issues in Britain[35] which includes Constantine being transformed into a seventy-year-old when exploring the theme of age.[36] She stated that it took her four days to get over the sight of herself aged so drastically, and compared the feeling to having an "electric shock".[8] Broadcasting was scheduled for November 7 2007 and the programme is newly entitled Trinny & Susannah Undress The Nation. On November 5 2007 Constantine and Woodall appeared on Good Morning America and performed makeovers on three women for the show and gave style advice according to the womens' shapes.[37] Constantine and Woodall have dressed over 5,000 women as of 2007.[38] With reference to their personal website, they state that their satisfaction remains with the many women they have helped and inspired and not so much with the success with book sales and viewing figures.

Constantine has appeared three times on Parkinson and made appearances on This Morning and Star in a Reasonably-Priced Car which is a recurring segment on the BBC Two motoring programme Top Gear, amongst numerous other talk shows. In 2003, when Constantine and Woodall were interviewed for the first time on Parkinson, actress Meg Ryan had a controversial interview with Michael Parkinson, which resulted in negative publicity for Ryan. Parkinson said that he felt Ryan's behaviour to his fellow guests Constantine and Woodall, whom she turned her back on, was "unforgivable".[39]

Beyond television work

To coincide with Constantine's move from the BBC to ITV1 in 2006, she co-launched an underwear range called "Trinny and Susannah Magic Pants" which are made of Nylon, produced to flatten the tummy, buttocks and thighs so that the areas appear slimmer.[40] Constantine even wore them after giving birth to her third child.[41] She currently co-writes a weekly column for The Sun with Woodall.

On December 14, 2007 it was announced that Constantine and Woodall have been contracted to embark upon a tour to Australia where they will make a series of public appearances at shopping malls owned by the Westfield Group.[42] During the tour, which will include visits to Melbourne and Sydney, they will be giving fashion advice to customers at the Westfield centres.[43]

Littlewoods

Constantine became the face of the home shopping company, Littlewoods Direct, with Woodall in 2006 as during its sponsorship of their ITV programme Trinny & Susannah Undress, orders quickly rose by thirty per cent.[44] From January 2007, the pair produced a twelve page fashion advice section within the Littlewoods catalogue and made a booklet called The Golden Rules, which was distributed to all Littlewoods customers with fashion advice to suit a range of body shapes.[44] They have produced online guidelines for Littlewoods, where customers are able to key in body measurements and get advice on clothing according to their figure.[44]

Constantine and Woodall made their first appearance in television advertisements to promote Littlewoods in April 2007.[45] The Mission:Impossible-themed advertisements feature Constantine and Woodall as themselves dressed as two agents trying to rob a Littlewoods designer warehouse.[46] The £12 million advertising campaign is one of largest ever for a home shopping and internet-based company.[47] Since the campaign began, Littlewoods' sales have risen by 18 per cent, with brand awareness augmenting by 13 per cent, followed by 56 per cent more customers visiting their website.[48] The recent Christmas adverts show them trying to steal a high-tech sleigh filled with Littlewoods designer presents.[49]

On September 20, 2007, Constantine and Woodall launched their own exclusive Littlewoods Autumn/Winter women's range which consists of trousers, coats and tops and, like their underwear range, are designed to shrink the buttocks, reduce thighs, flatten tummies or create waist definition.[5] A series of eight dresses, cashmere knitwear, faux fur and sequinned shrugs also feature in the range.[50] Constantine and Woodall solely designed the clothing items, with Littlewoods advising them on pricing and selling.[51] She commented "If you want to create a waist, there's a dress that's going to do that for you too. We've designed it very much around the female body."[52] She also stated "It's not about what size you are, it's about how you can minimise or accentuate parts of your body with clothing. That's what we've created."[53] The range is currently available exclusively at Littlewoods Direct.[5]

Books

Constantine has co-written several style advice books with her fashion partner Trinny Woodall, some of which include What Not to Wear, What You Wear Can Change Your Life, The Survival Guide and Trinny & Susannah Take on America which have sold a reputed 2.5 million copies worldwide.[4] The fashion advice books have been number one bestsellers in Britain and the United States, appearing on both The Sunday Times bestseller list[54] and The New York Times bestseller list,[55] and have been translated throughout the world.[56]

Their most successful book to date, What Not to Wear, was published in 2002 which displayed striking chapters such as "Big Tits", "No Tits" and "Big Bum" with fashion advice for each category.[57] It became an instant bestseller with total sales reaching 670,000 copies, consequently outselling Jamie Oliver and Nigella Lawson.[3][58] Before the prime book selling season, their own book had sold 250,000 copies in Britain.[58] The book was at one point selling 45,000 copies each week and sold 300,000 copies in just fifteen weeks,[59] eventually making sales worth £8.7 million.[57] Other success with the book includes winning a British Book Award in 2003 for the TV & Film Book of the Year.[2] Despite the book becoming an instant hit, Constantine and Woodall were only given an advance of £10,000.[58] It was then reported that Constantine and Woodall secured a £1 million book deal to write more of their fashion books.[60]

The fashion duo's latest book, The Body Shape Bible, was published on September 18, 2007. Through a survey about different body shapes conducted amongst women, Constantine and Woodall were able to discover the twelve women's body shapes that appeared most frequently.[51] They used this information to write The Body Shape Bible which highlights these twelve shapes which are all named in ways such as 'cornet', 'brick' or 'goblet'.[51] An 'apple' for instance, is classed as a woman who is round but has breasts that are smaller than her stomach as the weight is carried around the middle.[51] In the fashion advice book, women can interpret which shape they are, and can then be given adequate fashion advice on their own individual shape.[8] A few pages in the book are also devoted to illustrating some of Constantine and Woodall's own fashion disasters.[52]

Personal life

Constantine grew up in an old priory in the Leicestershire village of Knipton and went hunting from the age of seven.[61] She had a close relationship with her father, Joseph Constantine, who was successful in the property and shipping sectors, was an ex-Eton pupil and served in the Coldstream Guards.[61] When she was young, Constantine would rely on her father for fashion advice and has commented that any style that she has, she learned from him. He was a talented artist and was offered worldwide art exhibitions, although too modest to accept. His death came suddenly and was a landmark for Constantine.[18]

Constantine became a fixture in 1980s British gossip columns and newspapers as the result of her relationship with Princess Margaret's son, David, Viscount Linley which lasted for eight years.[15] Princess Margaret thought highly of Constantine and even called her "my daughter-in-law".[61] Constantine admitted that her relationship with Linley undeniably opened doors for her, but after they broke up, she was keen to put the episode behind her and become well-known in her own right.[12] She has also had a relationship with Pakistan cricket captain Imran Khan, but she is now married to Danish entrepreneur and businessman Sten Bertelsen, who launched Death cigarettes,[12] and with whom she has three children; Joe, Esme and CeCe.[5][10] Lulu is godmother to one of her children.[62] Constantine and her family have recently bought a 120-acre property in Sussex.[5] Constantine is estimated to be worth £5 million.[63]

After having her first child, Constantine suffered from severe postnatal depression[61] and panic attacks.[12] Upon discovering that she was pregnant for a third time, as well as being overcome with delight, she felt despair as it came at a time when best friend Trinny Woodall was struggling to conceive and undergoing IVF treatment. In an interview Constantine said that she "couldn't bear to tell her." A short time later however, Woodall became pregnant.[61]

In 2002, when on a visit to Cannes film festival, Constantine and Woodall were the targets of gem thieves. The thieves broke into the villa on the French Riviera where they were staying, rendered Constantine and Woodall unconscious with chloroform, and then continued to steal money and jewellery.[64] Carol Vorderman was involved in a feud with Constantine and Woodall in 2003. Vorderman commented harshly about the double-act, after they had called her an "overdone Eighties nightmare" and named Vorderman in their list of the 20 worst-dressed celebrities.[65]

Parodies

On the show Big Impression, impressionist Ronni Ancona took to spoofing her presenting techniques on What Not to Wear,[13][66] just one of the shows in which Constantine's character has been spoofed. She has also been referred to on the comedy sketch show French & Saunders as being a "bully" in a Celebrity Grading Report, where Dawn French was a headmaster of a celebrity school and had to write comments on various celebrities. Similarly, Constantine and Trinny Woodall were featured in a cartoon strip in the comic Viz where they were portrayed as school bullies who targeted victims who wore NHS glasses and hand-me-down clothing.[67] When Constantine and Woodall saw the cartoon strip, they threatened to take legal action against the comic.[68]

She was also spoofed in a sketch on 2DTV when it featured her giving Santa Claus a makeover, substituting his traditional red suit for a casual shirt and trousers.[69] She was also portrayed as a lesbian with Woodall on Avid Merrion's show Bo' Selecta!. Even though she was lampooned on the show, Constantine took the portrayal as a compliment.[70] In 2006, on Gordon Ramsay's The F-Word, Ramsay named his two pigs Trinny and Susannah.[71] Despite the worries of Ramsay's wife, the duo found the naming of the pigs humorous.[32]
