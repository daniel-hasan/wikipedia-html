{{Infobox Album}} Christ Illusion is the ninth studio album by the American thrash metal band Slayer. Released on August 8, 2006, the album received generally favorable critical reviews, and it entered the Billboard 200 at number five—the band's highest U.S. chart position to date. Christ Illusion includes the Grammy Award-winning "Eyes of the Insane", and is the band's first album to feature original drummer Dave Lombardo since 1990's Seasons in the Abyss.

Depicting a mutilated Christ painted by longtime collaborator Larry Carroll, the album's graphic artwork courted controversy. An alternative cover was issued to conservative retailers who felt uncomfortable with the original. Lyrical themes, particularly in the song "Jihad" which describes the September 11th attacks from a terrorist perspective, outraged the Mumbai-based Catholic Secular Forum in India. As a result, all Indian stocks of the album were recalled and destroyed by EMI India.

Recording

Christ Illusion was recorded via computer between two studios: NRG Studios, North Hollywood, with assistant engineer Dave Colvin; and Westlake Studios, Los Angeles, where Brian Warwick assisted.[1][2] Guitarist Kerry King recalls nine of the eleven songs originally slated for Christ Illusion were demoed in 2004, with Dave Lombardo at the drum kit.[3] However, Lombardo recalls recording with King as far back as early 2003, when two demos were completed at Lombardo's home.[4] In a July 2004 interview, guitarist Jeff Hanneman reported "Me and Kerry have a bunch of songs", and expressed the band's intent to finish the record that year.[5] Slayer's label American Recordings was at the time negotiating a switch of record distributors from Columbia Records to Warner Bros. Records, meaning the recording would have been temporarily shelved until the issue became resolved.[5] For this reason the group did not finish a record at that time,[5] as the distribution deal with Warner Records was not finalized until late July 2005.[6]

Slayer wished for Reign in Blood producer Rick Rubin to produce the album, and expected him to do so after Rubin expressed an initial interest.[7] Rubin was busy, however, which caused a further delay to recording.[7] While Christ Illusions recording was finally underway Rubin lent production to Metallica's untitled ninth studio album, an action later described by King as a "slap in the fucking face".[7] Josh Abraham produced the album instead,[1] and was praised by Blabbermouth's Don Kaye "for capturing much more of the spark than has been apparent on the last few records".[8] Despite missing an opportunity to produce Christ Illusion, Rubin contributed in an "executive production" capacity.[1] King was critical of his involvement, and said he cannot recall Rubin's presence in the studio during the recording, and that Rubin's main contribution was in providing suggestions during the final mix.[2] Jamie Thomson of UK's The Guardian newspaper was scornful of Rubin's contribution, and observed Slayer "seem unwilling to ditch the nu-metal tendencies that have made much of their recent output so resistible, which suggests Rubin's involvement was considerably less hands-on than in his remarkable redemptions of Johnny Cash and Neil Diamond."[9]

As with Slayer's previous two albums, all rhythm guitar tracks on Christ Illusion were laid down by King.[2] Using a Marshall JCM 800 as the main guitar sound throughout the album, King wrote roughly 80% of his guitar solo parts prior to the sessions.[2] The song "Catalyst", meanwhile, almost saw inclusion on Slayer's 2001 album God Hates Us All, existing in an alternative version which features former drummer Paul Bostaph on drums.[3] Lombardo's involvement marked the first time he, King, Araya and Hanneman had appeared together on record since the release of Seasons in the Abyss in 1990,[2] a reason cited by guitarist Jeff Hanneman for an alleged clearer punk vibe throughout the songs on the album.[10] Lombardo personally described the album as "a matured Reign in Blood",[11] while King described it as "a mix between God Hates [Us All] and Seasons [in the Abyss]."[12]

Although 11 songs were originally slated for the album, only ten made the final track listing. A song penned by Hanneman,[3] entitled "Final Six", was meant for inclusion,[2] with the song name originally declared as the album's title by vocalist Tom Araya to George Stroumboulopoulos of CBC's The Hour.[13] Questioned about the album's title by Kevin and Bean of Los Angeles KROQ-FM, King replied "I'm not positive it's been nailed yet. I think last week was the deadline and I'm not sure where it ended up, so I'm gonna have to take the fifth on that one. The way it was going, my vote was overlooked, so… That's why I'm not real thrilled about it."[13] However, Araya took one weekend off for vacation during the recording of the album and required a two-hour gall bladder operation the following Monday on May 5, 2006.[2][14] Thus, he was unable to finish the vocals for the song in time for the album's release.[2] King hinted "Final Six" may appear on a special digipack release of Christ Illusion[2], a release which eventually surfaced in July 2007.

Marketing and promotion

{{Sound sample box align right}} {{listen}} {{sample box end}} Christ Illusion was originally scheduled for release on June 6, 2006;[3] the sixth day of the sixth month of the sixth year. This connotation with the Book of Revelation's Number of the Beast was being used as a marketing ploy to hype a number of media releases at the time, most notably the remake of the horror film The Omen.[15] King said the idea was scrapped because of the number of other bands that had the same idea,[3] but USA Today reported that the release date was thwarted because the band had failed to secure sufficient studio recording time.[15] Having missed the "Satanic" date, the release was pushed back to July 25,[13] however this date was not met either. Despite this, an exclusive T-shirt, limited to 666 units and only available via the band's store, was released in commemoration of "the sixth day of the sixth month of the sixth year".[14] Five thousand copies of the limited edition EP "Eternal Pyre" were also released on this date, and made available via Hot Topic stores in the US. The EP previewed the song "Cult", and the track was made available for streaming on the band's official website the same day.[16] Issued in Europe on June 23, the EP landed at number 48 on the Swedish charts[17] and number 2 on the Finnish charts,[18] while on June 30, Nuclear Blast Records released a 7" vinyl picture disc version limited to a thousand copies.[19]

Not all media attention surrounding the group on June 6 was favourable. National Day of Slayer, LLC, which describes itself as a "a non-profit corporation in the State of Wyoming", requested on their website that Slayer fanatics participate in "The National Day of Slayer" by coming together and listening to the group's tracks.[20] However, vandals attacked St. Joseph's Seminary in Yonkers, New York by spray-painting a large pentagram in front of the doors; black inverted crosses in two columns in front of the main entrance; and the number six on three steps leading into the Seminary. The words "Reign in Blood" were scrawled on the seminary landing,[21] while the phrase "Better to reign in Hell than to serve in Heaven", taken from Book 1 of John Milton's poem "Paradise Lost", was found inscribed on two inside columns.[21] The National Day of Slayer website took credit for inspiring the perpetrator(s), and a media investigation discovered that the site had left instructions that fans "spray paint SLAYER logos on churches, synagogues, or cemeteries".[22]

In response to the ban, Christ Illusion promotional posters read "We can't show you the artwork, radio can't play this record, TV can't run our advert. To see this record you're gonna have to buy it"
Fans were given an exclusive preview of further tracks culled from the upcoming album before its release. In addition to "Cult", the tracks "Jihad" and "Eyes of the Insane" were made available for streaming on the Spanish website Rafabasa.com in late June.[23] A listening party event for the album took place on July 22 at Duff's Brooklyn in Williamsburg, New York.[24] Filmed on the set of "The Henry Rollins Show", a live rendition of "Disciple" (taken from Slayer's 2001 album God Hates Us All) was posted online, followed by "Cult"'s live performance which aired on the Independent Film Channel a few days later.[25] BBC Radio 1's "Mike Davis Rock Show" gave "Skeleton Christ" a premier airing on August 1,[26] and by August 4 the full album was available for streaming via Slayer's official MySpace profile.[27] AOL Radio complimented this by launching an "All Slayer" station in anticipation of Christ Illusions release, playing all of Slayer's previously released songs and tracks from the upcoming record.[28]

In late July 2006, bus benches in several Californian cities were decorated with promotional artwork for Christ Illusion.[29] City officials in Fullerton, California demanded the artwork be immediately removed from seventeen bus benches located throughout the city, and contacted the hired company which had originally put the adverts in place to assume the task.[29] The officials disliked the band's name, which they felt referred to a murderer. They also took offense to the antichrist and skull logo adorning the bench artwork.[29] Eventually, the artwork was removed.[30] However, various Orange County, California areas surrounding the city of Fullerton still had benches sporting the cover artwork.[30]

Critical reception

Christ Illusion was released on August 8, 2006 by American Recordings / Warner Bros. Records. In its first week of release, the album sold 62,000 copies in the United States and debuted at number 5 on the Billboard 200 Chart.[31] Though this ranked as the band's highest chart position to date, and was their first top 10 charting since 1994's Divine Intervention,[31] the album dropped to number 44 the following week.[32] Christ Illusion reached number 9 in Australia,[33] number 3 in Canada, number 6 in Austria, number 8 in the Netherlands, number 10 in Norway and debuted at number 2 in Finland[34] and Germany.[35] The single "Eyes of the Insane" won the "Best Metal Performance" category at the 49th Grammy Awards.[36] The song "Final Six" received a Grammy nomination at the 50th Grammy Awards.[37]

The album was met with generally favourable reviews. Thom Jurek of All Music Guide hailed the album as "raging, forward-thinking heavy metal melding with hardcore thrash", and wrote that Christ Illusion marked a return to "what made them such a breath of fresh air in the first place."[38] Ben Ratliff of the New York Times described the album as possessing "a kind of demented gravity, and the music bears it out: it is the most concentrated, focused Slayer record in 20 years."[39] PopMatters critic Adrien Begrand called it "Slayer's best album in sixteen years and their most thought-provoking work to date",[40] and the album was placed at number 15 on PopMatters list of The Best Metal Albums of 2006.[41] Drummer Lombardo came in for particular praise; though Rolling Stone slated the album, the reviewer acknowledged that "at least their awesome drummer Dave Lombardo shows off some chops."[42] Blabbermouths Don Kaye thought that "while flawed", Christ Illusion "proves that the band still has a few tricks up its sleeve and one very potent weapon behind the kit."[8] Peter Atkinson of KNAC.com felt similarly, and reported Lombardo's "performance is top notch throughout and does give the album a looser feel than Paul Bostaph’s technical precision offered."[43]

Not all critics were so positive. Chris Steffen of Rolling Stone magazine dismissed the album, noting that it "mines much of the same territory as its predecessor, God Hates Us All, just without the memorable riffs."[42] Jamie Thomson of The Guardian described the album as "wholly disappointing", and thought the band sounded "unwilling to ditch the nu-metal tendencies that have made much of their recent output so resistible."[9] KNAC.com contributor Peter Atkinson felt that the album "demands OUTRAGE —more calculatingly so than any other album the band has done", "and that, in a nutshell, is Christ Illusion’s glaring weakness."[43]

Album artwork and lyrical themes

Several aspects of Christ Illusions content and promotion generated adverse attention and publicity. In particular the cover art, painted by Larry Carroll and depicting a mutilated, stoned Jesus, coupled with song lyrics which dealt with terrorism, warfare and religion, drew criticism from conservative groups.

Album artwork

The censored album cover for Christ Illusion, 2006
Carroll, who had painted the cover artwork to previous Slayer albums such as Reign in Blood, South of Heaven and Seasons in the Abyss, resumed duties on Christ Illusion.[44] Working solely from track names and formative lyrics, Carroll produced the original on a 4-by-4-foot slab of wood using a combination of mediums.[44] Having requested an image of Christ in "a sea of despair", King commented that an early version seemed as though Christ was "chilling out in the water".[45] The final image portrays Christ with a missing eye and amputated hands, and standing amidst a sea of blood and severed heads.[44] Araya deemed this version "much better because he looked like a drug addict!",[45] while King admired the artwork enough to purchase the original.[46] Certain album pre-orders gave fans the chance to win one of ten autographed lithographs of the artwork,[47] while an alternative, non-graphic cover was made to appease retailers who had refused to stock the original version.[48]

World Entertainment News Network reported Slayer were attracting controversy through issuing the artwork.[45] Joseph Dias of the Mumbai Christian group Catholic Secular Forum took "strong exception" to the original album artwork, and issued a memorandum to Mumbai's police commissioner in protest.[49] Chris Steffen of Rolling Stone magazine commented that "The album art takes it all over the top with an image axeman Kerry King dubs 'Christ in a Sea of Despair'",[42] while KNAC.coms Peter Atkinson deemed the artwork "defiantly sacrilegious".[43]

Lyrical themes

Lyrical themes explored on Christ Illusion include a depiction of the September 11 attacks from the viewpoint of one of the terrorists ("Jihad"),[50] and a portrayal of a soldier's experience of post-traumatic stress ("Eyes of the Insane").[51] The song "Cult" revolves around King's perception of flaws in American religion,[52] while "Consfearacy" has been described as a "government hating song".[53][54]

{{Sound sample box align right}} {{listen}} {{sample box end}} Critical reaction to the album's lyrical content was mixed. Thom Jurek of All Music Guide felt the "dark, unrelentingly twisted-as-fuck lyrics reflect a singular intensity", and praised the band for connecting their anti-religion stance with a belief that religion has underscored many wars throughout history.[38] However, Rolling Stones Chris Steffen mourned that it had become "downright painful to hear Tom Araya — at 45 years old! — continue to belt out the band's increasingly self-parodying, anti-religious lyrics", and singled out lyrics such as "Religion's a whore" and "I've made my choice: six six six!" as over the top.[42] Jamie Thomson of The Guardian wrote that the album left "no blasphemy...unuttered",[9] while Peter Atkinson of KNAC.com observed that "when not fixating on religion, the band revisit their other favorite subject — war — in surprisingly familiar terms", and remarked that Slayer had sunk "to the level of God-repelling dunderheads Deicide."[43] He concluded the review with the opinion that "It's déjà vu all over again from God Hates Us All — and once you've titled something God Hates Us All, haven't you made your point enough already?"[43]

A Christian group, the Mumbai based Catholic Secular Forum (CSF), condemned the album's lyrical content. The organisation's general secretary Joseph Dias issued a statement in which he deemed the lyrics to "Skeleton Christ" to be an "insult to Christianity".[49] The memorandum was sent to Mumbai's police commissioner, and further expressed concern that the track "Jihad" would offend "the sensibilities of the Muslims...and secular Indians who have respect for all faiths."[49] EMI India met with the CSF, apologising for Christ Illusions release and recalling the album with no plans of a reissue.[49] On October 11, 2006 it was announced all stocks had been destroyed.[55] Though Araya had expected "Jihad"'s treatment of the events of 9/11 to create an American backlash,[50] it failed to materialise, in part, he believes, due to peoples' view that the song is merely "just Slayer being Slayer".[10]
