Parry Aftab is a lawyer specializing in Internet privacy and security law, and is the Executive Director of WiredSafety.org, a volunteer organization dedicated to online safety. Aftab shut down her law practice in 2000 and now only takes a limited number of consulting cases a year. She was featured in Chris Hansen's book, To Catch a Predator as one of two advocates in the area of online safety, in his chapter "The Tale of Two Advocates". In this chapter, Hansen highlights the reasons Parry gave up her law firm and donates her time running WiredSafety.org (an all-volunteer organization, including Parry). The first was the result of her having encountered an online image of a toddler being sexually molested. The second was because she recognized that "all children can walk, talk and see" online.(see Hansen's book.)

Parry began working in the area of online safety in 1997, after appearing on CNN and other shows defending the Internet from regulations designed to censor it. Her work quickly expanded to helping educators [1]

Education

  • City University of New York, Hunter College, BA, 1981 (receiving her degree in two years) [2]
  • Hunter College, Valedictorian, Phi Beta Kappa (Nu Chapter) [2]
  • New York University School of Law, JD, 1984 [2]

Consulting and advisory activities

She consults for and works with law enforcement agencies, worldwide, on cybercrime prevention, cyber-terrorism, law enforcement and security matters. [3] She has worked with FBI, U.S. Customs, Interpol, Scotland Yard, the Home Office, Tokyo Metropolitan Police and Japan's National Police, Hong Kong Police and the RCMP.[4][5]

Parry is a regular source to media outlets worldwide on Internet privacy, marketing, advertising and regulatory issues, as well as children and online safety and security.[6] [7] She has been quoted or featured in magazines such as Newsweek, U.S News & World Report, Family Circle, Good Housekeeping, Reader's Digest, Better Homes and Gardens, Information Weekly, Teen People, Ladies Home Journal, Smart Business Magazine, The Times magazine, New York Magazine, Cosmopolitan Magazine. [8][9] She has been consulted by local news publications and has appeared as a commentator on international, national, and local television news programs. [10][11]

She has been providing public service safety information for many years. [5] Parry provides parent Internet education and online safety content for such sites as Children's Television Workshop, Disney, Microsoft, Oracle, Verizon, AT&T and MSNBC and many of the social networking sites. [12]

Her online discussions and writing are have been published and quoted by legal journals in articles and judges in their decisions. [13]

Katie.com

{{main}}

Aftab was involved in a dispute surrounding the domain katie.com in 2004. In 2000, Penguin Putnam had planned to release a book titled Katie.com; the domain was owned by Katie Jones, which resulted in a dispute between her and the publisher.[14] Four years later, Aftab contacted Jones and, in an effort to address concerns relating to young people visiting Jones' site, thinking it was the official book site, asked Jones to consider either donating the site or redirecting traffic from the young readers to the official site. When Jones refused, Aftab accused her of having a hidden agenda, which Jones considered to be cyberbullying in itself.[15] In an interview, Jones stated that she was being emotionally blackmailed and that Aftab told her that "things would only get worse [for Jones]" if she did not hand over the domain.[16] Jones received massive support from the online community,[16][14] and eventually Penguin renamed the book A Girl's Life Online.

Publications and related activities

She has written the books The Parent's Guide to Protecting Your Children in Cyberspace (McGraw-Hill, January 2000 (US) and 2001 (UK)) and A Parents' Guide to the Internet (SC Press, October 1997). She has also published a new book for parents in Spain and in South and Central America,

Aftab has contributed to the books Child Abuse on the Internet.... Ending the Silence (2001, editor Carlos A. Arnaldo), The Technical Response: Blocking, Filtering And Rating The Internet, The Best In E-Commerce Law (2001, Warren E.Agin), and was the editor of Children's Online Privacy Law.

Her writings have been published in Information Week, New York Law Journal, Legal Times, Preventive Law Reporter, Working Women Magazine, The Texas Lawyer, Children's Legal Rights Law Journal, New Jersey Law Journal, and The Internet Lawyer.{{Fact}}

Aftab has been featured in Reader's Digest (US edition), TV Guide (US edition), Biography Magazine, New York Sunday Times, and the International Herald Tribune.{{Fact}}
